Welcome everyone to LEAP 2026. Today we are diving deep into the architecture that is revolutionizing time series analysis: The Transformer. We will move beyond basic intuition into the technical mechanisms that allow these models to scale.

The Unique Friction of Time

  • Non-Stationarity: Statistics (mean, variance) change over time.
  • Long Dependencies: An event 3 years ago affects today.
  • Irregular Sampling: Missing data, varying intervals.

Distribution Shift Detected

Time series data isn't like text or images. It drifts. The statistical properties of financial data in 2020 are different from 2025. Standard models struggle to adapt to these shifts without constant retraining.

Why Classical Methods Fail at Scale

ARIMA / Exponential Smoothing

Great for univariate, linear patterns.
Fail: High-dimensionality, non-linear interactions.

LSTMs / RNNs

Handle sequences well.
Fail: Sequential processing prevents parallelization. "Forgetting" long contexts.

Sequential Bottleneck

O(N) training time

ARIMA is mathematically beautiful but fails on complex web-scale data. LSTMs were the king of deep learning, but they process tokens one by one. You cannot train them on billions of points efficiently.

Transformer Intuition: Global View

Unlike RNNs, Transformers see the entire sequence at once.

The core revolution is 'Attention'. Every point in time looks at every other point in time simultaneously to decide what is relevant. It's O(1) path length between any two events.

Time Series Tokenization: Patching

Raw time points contain little semantics. We aggregate them into Patches.

1. Local semantic: Captures shape info.
2. Compute efficiency: Reduces sequence length by stride factor (e.g., L/8).

In NLP, a word is a token. In Time Series, a single number (e.g., temperature at 12:01) has no meaning. We group time steps into 'patches' (like visual patches in ViT). This reduces computational cost and adds semantic density.

Injecting Time: Positional Encoding

Transformers are permutation invariant. We must add position info.

P(pos, 2i) = sin(pos / 10000^(2i/d))
Since the model processes everything in parallel, it doesn't know 'yesterday' came before 'today' unless we tell it. We add a signal to the embedding. Sinusoidal allows extrapolation; Learnable is flexible; Timestamp uses actual dates.

Self-Attention Mechanism

$$ Attention(Q, K, V) = softmax(\frac{QK^T}{\sqrt{d_k}})V $$

This is the engine room. Queries look for Keys. If they match (dot product), the value is retrieved. Hover over the matrix: see how one time step focuses intensely on a specific past time step (like looking at last year's holiday sales to predict this year's).

Multi-Head Attention: capturing Patterns

Different heads learn different temporal dynamics.

Head 1: Short-term (Daily)

Head 2: Long-term (Trend)

Why multi-head? Because time series have multiple frequencies. One head attends to daily patterns (yesterday), another attends to weekly patterns (last Tuesday), another to yearly trends.

Stability: Norm & Feed-Forward

Layer Normalization
Feed Forward (MLP)
Residual Connection (Add)

Why is this crucial for TS?

  • Reversible Instance Norm (RevIN): Removes non-stationarity shift before processing, adds it back after.
  • MLP: Processes the "state" extracted by attention.
Normalization is critical. RevIN (Reversible Instance Normalization) is a standard trick in TS transformers: we subtract mean/var before the network and add it back at the end to handle distribution shift.

Pretraining Objective: Masked Patch Modeling

Self-Supervised Learning (SSL): Hide a patch, ask model to reconstruct it.

How do we train without labels? We mask parts of the signal (make them zero or random) and force the Transformer to use context to fill in the blanks. This forces it to learn the physics of the data.

Common Failure Modes

Context Window Limits

Despite linear attention tricks, extremely long history (>10k) is still heavy.

Distribution Shift

If the test data physics are fundamentally alien to the pre-training corpus, zero-shot fails.

Inference Latency

Huge models are slow. Not suitable for HFT (High Frequency Trading) yet.

They aren't magic. They are heavy, slow compared to XGBoost, and if the distribution shifts too wildly (like COVID-19 impact), they can hallucinate patterns.
